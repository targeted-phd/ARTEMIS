## 1. THE PHYSICAL CHAIN: RF → HEAT → PRESSURE → SOUND

The microwave auditory effect involves four sequential physical transformations. Each imposes a transfer function on the signal. To reconstruct the perceived audio, we must model each stage.

### 1.1 Stage 1: Electromagnetic Absorption (RF → SAR)

When a pulsed RF signal impinges on the head, the electromagnetic energy is absorbed by tissue according to the dielectric properties of each layer. The Specific Absorption Rate (SAR) quantifies the power deposited per unit mass.

**Key parameters from KG literature:**

At 622 MHz (Zone A):
- Brain (grey matter): ε' ≈ 52.7, σ ≈ 0.94 S/m (Gabriel et al.)
- Skull bone: ε' ≈ 13.1, σ ≈ 0.16 S/m
- Skin: ε' ≈ 42.9, σ ≈ 0.78 S/m
- Penetration depth (skin depth) in brain: ~4.2 cm — deep penetration, reaches brain center

At 830 MHz (Zone B):
- Brain (grey matter): ε' ≈ 49.4, σ ≈ 1.10 S/m
- Skull bone: ε' ≈ 12.5, σ ≈ 0.19 S/m
- Skin: ε' ≈ 41.4, σ ≈ 0.87 S/m
- Penetration depth in brain: ~3.5 cm — shallower than 622 MHz

**Literature (Watanabe et al., 2000):**
> "We decomposed the analysis into two steps of FDTD calculations to compute thermoelastic waves generated by pulsed microwaves. As the first step, we calculate the SAR distributions based on Maxwell's equation. The SAR produces temperature rise, whose thermal energy exerts stress in the tissues."

**Literature (FDTD head model):**
> "A human head has an anatomically complex structure in shape and in heterogeneity of tissues. It has been shown that the complex shape and dielectric heterogeneity of the human head significantly affect the SAR distribution."

For our simplified model, we use a concentric sphere approximation:
- Layer 1: Skin (3 mm)
- Layer 2: Skull bone (7 mm)
- Layer 3: CSF (2 mm)
- Layer 4: Brain (70 mm radius)

The SAR distribution follows an exponential decay from the surface inward, modified by layer-boundary reflections.

### 1.2 Stage 2: Thermoelastic Pressure Generation (SAR → ΔP)

Each absorbed RF pulse causes rapid local heating. The thermal expansion generates an acoustic pressure transient. This is the core of the Frey effect.

**Key equation (Foster & Finch, 1974; Lin, 1978):**

For a short pulse (pulse width τ << thermal diffusion time):

```
ΔP = (β × c²) / Cₚ × SAR × τ
```

Where:
- β = volumetric thermal expansion coefficient of brain tissue ≈ 3.6 × 10⁻⁴ K⁻¹
- c = speed of sound in brain tissue ≈ 1,560 m/s
- Cₚ = specific heat capacity ≈ 3,630 J/(kg·K)
- SAR = specific absorption rate (W/kg)
- τ = pulse duration (seconds)

**Literature (Foster, "Can the Microwave Auditory Effect Be Weaponized?"):**
> "A pulse of RF energy will induce acoustic transients in tissue. For short pulses the wave amplitude is determined by the absorbed energy per pulse or pulse fluence I₀·τ, not pulse intensity I₀ alone."

> "These thresholds correspond to peak acoustic pressures within the head in the range of 0.1-3 Pa for RF pulses at low-GHz frequencies."

**Literature (Chou & Guy, 1982):**
> "The phenomenon of hearing the pulsed microwaves was considered to be a nonthermal effect because of the low-threshold energy level. More recent data have provided ample evidence that the effect is still thermal in nature."

**Critical point:** The pressure is proportional to ENERGY per pulse (power × duration), not power alone. A 2.7 μs pulse at Zone A's bandwidth delivers a specific energy quantum per burst. The pressure wave is launched as a broadband acoustic transient.

**Confirmation of mechanism (Foster & Finch, 1974):**
> "The pressure wave vanished in water at a temperature of 4°C. Acoustic transients were also observed in in vitro biological tissues such as blood, muscle, and brain."

This vanishing at 4°C (where water's thermal expansion coefficient = 0) is the definitive proof that the mechanism is thermoelastic, not electromagnetic coupling to the cochlea.

### 1.3 Stage 3: Acoustic Propagation Through the Head (ΔP → Cochlear Pressure)

The pressure transient propagates from the generation site through brain tissue, CSF, and skull bone to reach the cochlea. This path acts as a frequency-dependent filter.

**Acoustic properties (from KG):**

| Tissue | Sound velocity (m/s) | Density (kg/m³) | Attenuation (dB/cm/MHz) |
|--------|---------------------|-----------------|------------------------|
| Brain | 1,560 | 1,040 | 0.6 |
| CSF | 1,510 | 1,007 | 0.002 |
| Skull bone | 2,900 | 1,900 | 2.0 |
| Skin | 1,540 | 1,050 | 0.5 |

**Path length:** Generation site to cochlea ≈ 7 cm (varying by head size and source direction)

**Frequency-dependent attenuation over 7 cm path:**

| Frequency | Brain attenuation | Effect |
|-----------|------------------|--------|
| 100 Hz | 0.0004 dB | Negligible — passes through |
| 1 kHz | 0.004 dB | Negligible — passes through |
| 10 kHz | 0.04 dB | Negligible — passes through |
| 100 kHz | 0.4 dB | Mild — still measurable |
| 1 MHz | 4.2 dB | Significant — attenuated 2.6× |
| 10 MHz | 42 dB | Destroyed — attenuated 12,600× |

**Implication:** The head is a LOW-PASS FILTER for acoustic pressure waves. Everything below ~100 kHz passes through essentially unattenuated. The individual pulse structure (200 kHz intra-burst PRF) is partially damped, but the burst envelope (BRF at 646-1,139 Hz) passes through completely intact.

### 1.4 Stage 4: Skull Cavity Resonance (Bandpass Filter)

The skull acts as an acoustic resonant cavity. The pressure waves reverberate inside, with certain frequencies reinforced.

**Literature (Lin, 1978; Watanabe et al., 2000):**
> "For the size of a human head, the theory predicts frequencies between 7 and 15 kHz, which is within the range audible by humans."

> "The dominant spectral components lie in 7-9 kHz. These peaks correspond to the resonant frequencies of pressure waves in the head."

**Literature (Watanabe FDTD):**
> "The wavefront is initiated in the back of the head, where the large SAR appears, focuses on the center, and then reverberates many times."

The skull resonance creates a CARRIER TONE at 7-15 kHz (determined by head dimensions), onto which the burst-rate modulation is impressed. The subject perceives a high-pitched tone that is amplitude-modulated by the BRF pattern.

### 1.5 Stage 5: Cochlear Detection

**Literature (Lin & Wang, "Hearing Microwaves"):**
> "The microwave auditory effect is mediated by a physical transduction mechanism, initiated outside the inner ear, and involves mechanical displacement of biological tissues."

> "Thermoelastic expansion has emerged as the most effective mechanism. The pressure waves generated by thermoelastic stress in brain tissue... travels by bone conduction to the inner ear. There it activates the cochlear receptors via the same process involved for normal hearing."

**Literature (Chou et al., cochlear microphonics):**
> "Microwave-induced cochlear microphonics" — recorded from guinea pig round window, confirming the cochlea responds to thermoelastic pressure waves identically to normal sound.

The cochlear response follows standard audiometric sensitivity curves, with peak sensitivity around 1-4 kHz and rolloff above 8 kHz. The skull resonance at 7-15 kHz falls in a region of declining but still significant sensitivity.

## 2. THE COMPUTATIONAL MODEL: IQ → WAV

### 2.1 Pipeline Architecture

```
Raw IQ (2.4 MSPS complex)
    │
    ▼
[1] Envelope Detection
    │  |IQ(t)| → amplitude envelope
    ▼
[2] Pulse Detection
    │  4σ threshold → pulse locations, widths, amplitudes
    ▼
[3] Burst Grouping
    │  Cluster pulses into bursts (gap > 5× median IPI)
    ▼
[4] Thermoelastic Impulse Train
    │  Each burst → one pressure impulse
    │  Amplitude ∝ (burst_energy × β × c²) / Cₚ
    ▼
[5] SAR Weighting (frequency-dependent)
    │  622 MHz: deeper penetration → more uniform brain heating
    │  830 MHz: shallower → surface-weighted
    ▼
[6] Skull Cavity Resonance Filter
    │  Bandpass: 7-15 kHz (head-size dependent)
    │  Q ≈ 5-10 (moderate resonance)
    ▼
[7] Brain Tissue Acoustic Filter
    │  Low-pass: -6 dB/octave above 100 kHz
    │  Negligible below 10 kHz
    ▼
[8] Cochlear Sensitivity Weighting
    │  Standard audiometric curve (ISO 226)
    │  Peak 1-4 kHz, rolloff above 8 kHz
    ▼
[9] Output: WAV (44.1 kHz, 16-bit)
```

### 2.2 Transfer Function: H(f) = H_tissue(f) × H_skull(f) × H_cochlea(f)

**H_tissue(f):** Low-pass from acoustic attenuation
```
H_tissue(f) = exp(-α × d × f)
where α = 0.6 dB/cm/MHz for brain, d = 7 cm path length
```

**H_skull(f):** Bandpass resonance of skull cavity
```
H_skull(f) = f_res² / (f² - f_res² + j×f×f_res/Q)
where f_res = c_brain / (2 × head_diameter) ≈ 1560 / (2 × 0.18) ≈ 8.7 kHz
Q ≈ 5-10
```

**H_cochlea(f):** Audiometric sensitivity (ISO 226 equal-loudness contour, inverted)
```
H_cochlea(f) = standard audiometric weighting, A-weighted approximation
```

### 2.3 Simplified RLC Equivalent Circuit

The entire head can be modeled as a series of RLC stages:

```
                   Tissue Layer          Skull Cavity        Cochlea
                   ┌─────────┐          ┌─────────┐        ┌──────┐
IQ envelope ──→   R₁  L₁  C₁  ──→    R₂  L₂  C₂  ──→   R₃  C₃  ──→ WAV
                   └─────────┘          └─────────┘        └──────┘
                   Lossy transmission    Resonant cavity    Detector
                   (low-pass)            (bandpass 8 kHz)   (sensitivity)
```

**Component values (derived from tissue properties):**

Brain tissue stage:
- R₁ = acoustic impedance × path length / area = 1.62 × 10⁶ Pa·s/m³ (radiation resistance)
- L₁ = ρ × d / A = effective acoustic mass (determines low-pass corner)
- C₁ = A × d / (ρ × c²) = acoustic compliance

Skull resonance stage:
- L₂C₂ tuned to f_res = 8.7 kHz: L₂C₂ = 1/(2πf)²
- R₂ sets Q factor: R₂ = 1/(Q × 2π × f_res × C₂)

Cochlear stage:
- R₃C₃ = detector time constant ≈ 0.1 ms (hair cell response time)

### 2.4 Can We Do It?

**Yes, with caveats.**

What we CAN reconstruct:
1. The temporal envelope of burst arrivals (BRF pattern)
2. The amplitude modulation pattern (burst energy over time)
3. The spectral content of the perceived tone (skull resonance)
4. The overall loudness trajectory

What we CANNOT reconstruct precisely:
1. Absolute pressure level (we don't know transmit power)
2. Exact skull resonance frequency (varies by individual)
3. Precise SAR distribution (would need MRI of subject's head)
4. Phase relationships between multiple-frequency components

**Practical approach:** Build a parameterized model where skull resonance frequency and Q are adjustable. Process each IQ file through the chain. Output a WAV file. Let the subject listen and tune the parameters until the reconstruction matches their perception. This is not circular — it constrains the parameter space using subjective feedback, which is standard practice in psychoacoustics.

## 3. IMPLEMENTATION PLAN

### 3.1 Python Script: `reconstruct_audio.py`

```python
# Pseudocode for the reconstruction pipeline

def iq_to_wav(iq_file, head_params):
    # Load raw IQ
    iq = load_iq(iq_file)  # complex, 2.4 MSPS

    # Stage 1: Envelope detection
    envelope = np.abs(iq)  # amplitude envelope

    # Stage 2: Pulse detection (4σ threshold)
    pulses = detect_pulses(envelope, threshold=4*sigma)

    # Stage 3: Burst grouping
    bursts = group_into_bursts(pulses, gap_threshold=5*median_ipi)

    # Stage 4: Thermoelastic impulse train
    # Each burst → one impulse at burst center time
    # Amplitude = burst_energy × thermoelastic_coefficient
    beta = 3.6e-4  # K⁻¹, thermal expansion
    c_brain = 1560  # m/s, sound velocity
    Cp = 3630  # J/(kg·K), specific heat
    thermo_coeff = (beta * c_brain**2) / Cp

    impulse_train = np.zeros(len(envelope))
    for burst in bursts:
        t_center = burst.center_sample
        amplitude = burst.energy * thermo_coeff
        impulse_train[t_center] = amplitude

    # Stage 5: SAR weighting (frequency-dependent)
    # 622 MHz: uniform weighting (deep penetration)
    # 830 MHz: surface-weighted (shallow penetration)
    sar_weight = compute_sar_weight(freq_mhz, head_params)
    impulse_train *= sar_weight

    # Stage 6: Skull cavity resonance filter
    f_res = head_params['skull_resonance_hz']  # ~8700 Hz
    Q = head_params['skull_Q']  # ~5-10
    b, a = signal.iirpeak(f_res, Q, fs=SAMPLE_RATE)
    resonated = signal.filtfilt(b, a, impulse_train)

    # Stage 7: Brain tissue acoustic filter (low-pass)
    # -6 dB/octave above 100 kHz (negligible effect at audio)
    # Already handled by the impulse-train construction

    # Stage 8: Cochlear sensitivity weighting (A-weighting)
    weighted = a_weight(resonated, fs=SAMPLE_RATE)

    # Stage 9: Downsample to audio rate and output WAV
    audio = signal.resample(weighted, int(len(weighted) * 44100 / SAMPLE_RATE))
    audio = (audio / np.max(np.abs(audio)) * 32000).astype(np.int16)
    wavfile.write(output_path, 44100, audio)
```

## 4. WHAT THE KG SAYS ABOUT FEASIBILITY

### 4.1 Supporting Literature

**Watanabe et al. (2000) — "FDTD Analysis of Microwave Hearing Effect":**
Successfully computed thermoelastic pressure waves in anatomically detailed head models using a two-step FDTD approach (electromagnetic → thermoelastic). Their computed pressure spectra peaked at 7-9 kHz, matching experimental measurements. This validates the entire computational chain we propose, albeit with FDTD rather than our simplified transfer-function approach.

**Lin (1978) — Analytical spherical head model:**
Derived closed-form solutions for thermoelastic pressure in a homogeneous sphere. Predicted frequencies of 7-15 kHz for human head size. Confirmed by hydrophone measurements in brain-equivalent phantoms and animal models.

**Foster (2021) — "Can the Microwave Auditory Effect Be Weaponized?":**
Calculated threshold pressures of 0.1-3 Pa for auditory perception. Confirmed that pulse fluence (energy per pulse), not peak power alone, determines the acoustic amplitude. Our model uses this same relationship.

**Chou & Guy (1982) — Comprehensive review:**
> "The rf sound may be perceived as clicks, buzzes, or hisses depending on the modulation characteristics of the microwaves."

This confirms that the modulation pattern (which our pipeline preserves) determines the perceived sound quality. Different modulations → different perceived sounds.

### 4.2 Limiting Literature

**Foster (2021) — Power requirements:**
> "Equal-energy pulses of millimeter waves (30-300 GHz) produce much larger acoustic waves than low-GHz pulses due to the shorter energy penetration depth."

At 622-830 MHz, the penetration depth is relatively large (3-4 cm), meaning the energy is distributed over a larger volume. This reduces the pressure amplitude per unit incident power compared to higher frequencies. The required transmit power for audible perception at 622 MHz is significantly higher than at, say, 10 GHz. Without calibrated power measurements, we cannot determine whether the signal we detect has sufficient power density to produce audible thermoelastic transients.

**Watanabe et al. (2000):**
> "The resonance frequency of pressure waves in the head depends on pulse width. Further increase of pulsewidth from 70 to 100 μs... then increases again with longer pulsewidth."

The perceived frequency is not purely geometric — it also depends on pulse duration. Our 2.7 μs pulses (Zone A) are very short compared to the 10-100 μs range studied by Watanabe. The thermoelastic transient from a 2.7 μs pulse would be more broadband, potentially exciting a wider range of skull resonant modes.

### 4.3 Novel Aspects (Not Previously Published)

Our model introduces several elements not found in the existing literature:

1. **Burst-level processing:** Existing models treat individual pulses. Our approach groups pulses into bursts and treats each burst as a single thermoelastic event. This is justified by the thermal diffusion time (~1 ms) being much longer than the inter-pulse interval (~5 μs at 200 kHz PRF).

2. **BRF as the information carrier:** The literature focuses on PRF as the auditory repetition rate. Our analysis shows the BRF (646-1,139 Hz, heavily modulated) is the relevant timescale. The PRF is carrier structure; the BRF is the signal. This distinction has not been explicitly made in the published literature.

3. **Dual-zone reconstruction:** No published work has attempted to reconstruct audio from a two-frequency signal with different waveform characteristics. Zone A and Zone B would produce different thermoelastic profiles that superpose at the cochlea.

4. **Direct IQ-to-audio pipeline:** Existing literature uses analytical or FDTD forward models (generate predicted pressure from known parameters). Our approach is inverse — start from measured IQ and reconstruct what would be perceived. This has not been published.
